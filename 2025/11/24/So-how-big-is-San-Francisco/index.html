So how big is San Francisco

2025-11-24

Context

My friend Daniel just started at Meta as a software engineer in San Francisco, so went to pay him a visit.

While he was showing me his neighbourhood and we were walking down from Telegraph Hill, he mentioned the city is “about 7 miles by 7 miles”. I found that an interesting fact and we left it at that.

On our way back, we checked the distance to get home using Google Maps, and it showed 0.51 miles. It felt a lot longer. Why? That’s because it was. Using Google’s point to point distance measuring tool, we found that the distance of 0.51 miles was as if we were walking on a flat surface. It didn’t account for the fact that we just crossed a steep hill.

In the measured profile of our route, you can see we climbed about 250 feet up, then descended about 170 feet down. Although the distance on the x axis shows 0.51 miles (about 2700 feet), the actual distance we walked is clearly longer.

This got us thinking, how big is San Francisco really? When we measure 7 miles, does this mean the straight line walking distance from one end of the city to the other? Or does it mean the distance if a laser went directly from one end to the other, ignoring all the hills and valleys in between?

How is surface measured?


Planar Area:

This is the most common way of measuring area. The terrain is reprojected onto a surface, effectively assuming a perfectly flat surface. Using this method means that an area would not increase in square footage if we added hills or valleys. This is what Google Maps does when it measures point to point distance, or distance on the route given by the GPS. In practice, this is the most common use case. When Uber charges you by distance or when you measure how far you ran on Strava, you’re using planar measurements.

The exact planar area of San Francisco is 46.92 square miles (US Census Bureau).

Geodesic Area:

To be slightly more accurate, geodesic measurement uses a smooth reference ellipsoid that mimics the Earth’s curvature. Just like a planar measurement, it ignores hill, valleys and roughness. That means measuring distances along the actual surface of the Earth, which is curved and uneven.

Terrain-aware (3D) Area:

This method takes into account the elevation changes of the terrain. It incorporates elevation by adding up true 3D segment lengths based on a given interval. For consecutive points (x1, y1, z1) and (x2, y2, z2), the segment length is sqrt((Δx)^2 + (Δy)^2 + (Δz)^2), accounting for hills and valleys via the change in elevation Δy and Δz. This makes the distance walked or area covered strictly larger than the planar measurement. This is useful for activities like hiking or mountain biking, where elevation changes significantly impact the distance traveled. For our example of going up and down Telegraph Hill, the actual distance would be calculated by summing all the hypotenuses of the lines in the measured profile using the Pythagorean theorem.

The coastline paradox

It may not be obvious, but we are stumbling into a classic problem in geography called the coastline paradox. The paradox is that the distance or area depends greatly on the granularity of the measurement. The more detailed the measurement, the longer (larger) the distance (area).

Let’s go back to our Telegraph Hill example. The two figures below show the measured profile of the exact same route. The difference is only in the resolution. The first uses 200’ sampling, whereas the second uses the more granular 25’ sampling. It’s visually obvious that the lower sampling (bottom figure) results in a longer line.

As we lower the sampling measurement, we are increasing the distance of that route. As we make the measurement infinitely small, the distance approaches infinity. The most famous example of this phenomenon is the coastline of Britain. Mappers have found that the length of Britain’s coastline increases without bound as the measurement scale decreases. This is because the coastline is highly irregular, with many small inlets and peninsulas that get smoothed out at higher intervals, but quickly add up when measured in finer detail.

Conclusion

To answer the question, “How big is San Francisco?”, I will give you the answer of any good economist: “It depends”.

From a theoretical standpoint, the area of San Francisco is infinite.
From a practical standpoint, let’s stick with 7x7 miles.
